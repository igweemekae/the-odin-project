Ofe Onugbu: Bitter leaf Soup

Description

Bitter leaf soup, also known as "Ofe Onugbu," is a rich and flavorful traditional Nigerian dish from the eastern part of the country,
harmoniously combining the earthy bitterness of the bitter leaf with assorted meats, traditional seasonings, and thickeners to create a hearty and comforting meal.

Ingredients

  • Fresh bitter leaves (1-2 bunches)
  • Assorted meats (such as beef, goat meat, and cow tripe)
  • Stockfish
  • Dry fish
  • Palm oil (1 cup)
  • Cocoyam (as a thickener) or a thickening powder (such as Achi or Ofo)
  • Ground crayfish (2 tablespoons)
  • Fresh peppers (such as Scotch Bonnet or Habanero)
  • Seasoning cubes
  • Salt (to taste)
  • Water
  • Ogiri (fermented locust bean paste or substitute)

Steps to Prepare Bitter leaf soup

Prepare the Bitter Leaves:
Rinse the bitter leaves thoroughly under running water to remove excess bitterness. This may involve several rinses or using lightly salted water to aid washing
Set the washed bitter leaves aside.

Cook the Meats:
In a large pot, add the assorted meats, stockfish, and dry fish. Season with salt, seasoning cubes, and chopped fresh peppers.
Add enough water to cover the meat and cook until the meats are tender.

Prepare the Cocoyam:
Boil the cocoyam until tender, then peel and pound into a smooth paste. This will be used to thicken the soup.

Combine the Ingredients:
Once the meats are cooked, add palm oil to the pot and allow it to incorporate with the broth.
Introduce the cocoyam paste in small lumps to the cooking broth, stirring gently to allow it to dissolve and thicken the soup.
Add the thoroughly washed bitter leaves, ground crayfish, and a spoonful of Ogiri.

Final Seasoning:
Stir and adjust seasoning with more salt or seasoning cubes to taste.
Allow the soup to simmer on low heat for an additional 10-15 minutes, stirring occasionally to ensure all elements are well combined.

Serve:
Once the soup has thickened and the flavors are harmonized, remove from heat and serve hot with traditional accompaniments such as pounded yam, fufu, or garri.
